Most Advanced Space Tech Right Now

Space tech is advancing at an incredible pace in 2025, driven by a combination of private innovation and government-backed programs! SpaceX’s Starship, the world’s most powerful rocket, just completed its eighth flight test in March 2025, showcasing its fully reusable design for lunar and Mars missions. Standing at 120 meters tall, Starship is designed to carry up to 150 metric tons to low Earth orbit, making it a game-changer for deep space exploration. SpaceX has plans to use Starship for its first crewed Mars mission as early as 2026, with Elon Musk aiming to establish a self-sustaining colony on the Red Planet within the next decade.

Meanwhile, NASA’s Artemis program is making significant strides toward sustainable lunar exploration. The Artemis III mission, scheduled for late 2026, will land the first humans on the moon since Apollo 17 in 1972. NASA is developing cutting-edge lunar rovers, such as the Volatiles Investigating Polar Exploration Rover (VIPER), which will search for water ice at the lunar south pole. Additionally, the Lunar Gateway—a small space station orbiting the moon—will serve as a staging point for future missions, including crewed trips to Mars. The Gateway’s Power and Propulsion Element (PPE) and Habitation and Logistics Outpost (HALO) modules are already under construction, with a planned launch in 2025.

Blue Origin’s New Glenn rocket is also making waves in the space industry. Named after astronaut John Glenn, this heavy-lift rocket stands 98 meters tall and is designed to carry 45 metric tons to low Earth orbit. Its reusable first stage, powered by seven BE-4 engines, aims to rival SpaceX’s Starship in cost-effectiveness. New Glenn’s first launch is targeted for late 2025, and Blue Origin has already secured contracts with NASA to deliver scientific payloads to the moon as part of the Artemis program. Jeff Bezos has emphasized that New Glenn will play a key role in Blue Origin’s vision of building infrastructure for a permanent human presence in space, including the Orbital Reef space station, a commercial project in collaboration with Sierra Space.

Other notable advancements include China’s Tiangong space station, which became fully operational in 2024, and the European Space Agency’s (ESA) reusable Space Rider, an uncrewed spacecraft set to launch in 2025 for low Earth orbit missions. These innovations are paving the way for a new era of space exploration, where international collaboration and competition are driving humanity closer to becoming a multi-planetary species. Stay tuned for more updates as these technologies continue to evolve!
